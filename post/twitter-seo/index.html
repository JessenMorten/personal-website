Drive traffic to your website using these meta tags in your HTML

SEO

February 28, 2022

Twitter Summary Cards offer a way to control how your content is presented when it's posted on Twitter, which can make more people want to visit your site.

Types of Twitter Cards

There are 4 types of Twitter Cards, each of which have their own purpose. We're gonna take a look at 2 of these types. Each Twitter Card type has a number of meta tags that you can add in the head section of your HTML, to let Twitter know how you want your content to be displayed.

Summary Card

The most straightforward and common Twitter Card, is the Summary Card. It displays a title, description and thumbnail.

html<head>
  <!-- Card type, required, must be set to a value of "summary" -->
  <meta name="twitter:card" content="summary">

  <!-- Title, required, a concise title for the related content -->
  <meta name="twitter:title" content="...">

  <!-- Site, optional, the Twitter @username the card should be attributed to -->
  <meta name="twitter:site" content="...">

  <!-- Description, optional, a description that concisely summarizes the content -->
  <meta name="twitter:description" content="...">

  <!-- Image, optional, A URL to a unique image representing the content of the page -->
  <meta name="twitter:image" content="...">

  <!-- Image alternative, optional, a text description of the image conveying the essential nature of an image to users who are visually impaired -->
  <meta name="twitter:image:alt" content="...">
</head>
Summary Card with Large Image

The next Twitter Card type is almost exactly like the previous type, except the image will be a large and full-width alongside a tweet.

html<head>
  <!-- Card type, required, must be set to a value of "summary_large_image" -->
  <meta name="twitter:card" content="summary_large_image">

  <!-- Title, required, a concise title for the related content -->
  <meta name="twitter:title" content="...">

  <!-- Site, optional, the Twitter @username the card should be attributed to -->
  <meta name="twitter:site" content="...">

  <!-- Description, optional, a description that concisely summarizes the content -->
  <meta name="twitter:description" content="...">

  <!-- Image, optional, a URL to a unique image representing the content of the page -->
  <meta name="twitter:image" content="...">

  <!-- Image alternative, optional, a text description of the image conveying the essential nature of an image to users who are visually impaired -->
  <meta name="twitter:image:alt" content="...">
</head>
Conclusion
